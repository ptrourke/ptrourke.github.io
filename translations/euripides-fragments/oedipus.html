NOTES

"Laios, king of Thebes, defied the oracle": Quoted in a handbook on Euripides (P. Oxy. 2455), which included titles, first lines, and summaries (hypotheses, in Greek) on all of his plays; about one third of the handbook survives (but very little survives intact).

"Then she sat, curling her tail": Fragment 540 Nauck2 , greatly augmented by P. Oxy. 2459. This sounds like a retrospective description of Oedipus̓ confrontation with the Sphinx, either by Iokaste or some minor figure explaining how Oedipus, putatively a Korinthian, came to rule in Thebes. Despite the stunning visual quality of the passage (which is obvious even through the mutilated papyrus-text), this is spoken verse, not lyric.

"We pushed him, Polybos' son, to the ground" Fragment 541 Nauck2, one of the most controversial fragments in the Euripidean corpus. This fragment boldly states that Oedipus was blinded by others, not by himself as in Sophokles̓ Oedipus the King; what is more, the fact that Oedipus is identified as Polybos̓ son, not Laios̓, implies that Oedipus was blinded before he was recognized as the child of Laios and Iokaste, that is, before his incest was discovered. (T. B. L. Webster, among others, discusses all the implications of this fragment in Tragedies of Euripides ; I have based my "framework" for the fragments on his discussion.)

"Shining silver and bright gold are not": Fragment 852 Nauck2

"A wife and children have a great hold on a man": Fragment 543 Nauck2

"A wife is the most formidable of enemies.": Fragment 544 Nauck2

"If a woman has any wisdom, she knows": Fragment 545 Nauck2

"All women are inferior": Fragment 546 Nauck2

"There is only one kind of desire": Fragment 547 Nauck2
